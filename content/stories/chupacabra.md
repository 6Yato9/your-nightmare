The first documented attacks were in Puerto Rico in 1995. Farmers in the municipality of Canóvanas found eight sheep dead in a field, each with three puncture wounds to the chest and completely drained of blood. No blood on the ground, no signs of a struggle, no tracks. Over the following weeks, more animals turned up — goats, chickens, rabbits — all killed the same way, all exsanguinated with surgical precision. A eyewitness named Madelyne Tolentino described the creature she saw near her home: about four feet tall, bipedal, with large oval red eyes, a row of spines down its back, and arms that ended in three-fingered clawed hands. It smelled, she said, of sulfur.

The name came almost immediately, from the wounds and the victims: the goat-sucker. *El chupacabra.* By the end of 1995, reports were coming in from across Puerto Rico, then from Mexico, then from Chile, Brazil, Colombia, and eventually Florida and Texas. Each region added details to the description. The mainland American version was often reported as more canine — a hairless dog-like animal running on all fours, with mange and large canine teeth. Whether these are the same creature, different creatures, or something in between remains unresolved.

What is consistent across every report, across thirty years and a dozen countries, is the method: the punctures, the complete drainage of blood, the absence of any mess. Veterinarians who examined the carcasses noted that this level of exsanguination is not characteristic of known predators. Known predators spill blood. They tear. They leave evidence of appetite. Whatever is doing this is not eating in any conventional sense.

The leading scientific explanation is that the carcasses are the work of known predators — coyotes, dogs, feral animals — whose kills are then misread by people who are already primed to see something extraordinary. The puncture wounds are canine teeth. The blood has been absorbed into the soil or consumed by insects before discovery. This is plausible and probably correct for many cases. It does not explain the cases where the animals were found indoors, in locked pens, with no signs of entry or exit.

The chupacabra is now a cultural phenomenon, appearing on merchandise and in films and in the names of barbecue restaurants. The farmers in Puerto Rico who first found their animals dead in 1995 would not recognize what it has become. For them, and for the communities that have continued to lose livestock in the decades since, it is not a mascot. It is something that comes at night and takes without leaving a trace, and they have not yet found out what it is.
