A firefly moving slowly across a darkened room is not alarming. It should be. Among the Ewe people of Ghana and Togo, the Adze is a vampire that walks in human form by day and transforms into a firefly at night — small, luminous, drifting through the gaps in walls and window frames that would stop anything larger. It is not a natural entity that became a vampire through some original curse. It is a human being who chose this. A sorcerer, or someone who acquired the power deliberately through dark ritual, who uses it to prey on sleeping people in their homes.

Its preference is for children, particularly infants, and the attack leaves no bite mark in the obvious sense — the Adze feeds through a kind of extraction that produces no wound, only the gradual decline. A child who was healthy yesterday is lethargic today and running a fever by the end of the week. This mimics the presentation of malaria, of childhood fevers, of a dozen ordinary diseases, which is how the Adze has operated undetected for centuries: not as a monster that comes in the night and leaves carnage, but as an invisible vector of illness, indistinguishable from the diseases that already killed children at high rates.

The identification of an Adze attack is therefore made through the pattern of deaths rather than any single incident. If children in a household or in a neighborhood die sequentially, with similar presentations of decline, the suspicion falls on a human agent. In traditional Ewe communities this suspicion was directed toward specific people who were known or suspected to have acquired supernatural power for personal use — the uncanny, the solitary, those who prospered strangely when their neighbors did not. The accusation of being an Adze practitioner was a serious and sometimes fatal social sanction.

If an Adze is caught in its firefly form, it cannot transform back into a human unless released. This is the leverage that, in theory, allows the community to compel a confession, to identify what the Adze took and from whom, to force the restoration of what was stolen. In practice, catching a firefly in a dark room requires knowing which firefly to catch, and the Adze has been selecting its moments for a very long time.
