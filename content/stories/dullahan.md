He rides at night, on a black horse that strikes sparks from the road, and he carries his own head tucked under one arm. The head is grey-green and the size of a man's head but with a wide, fixed grin that stretches from ear to ear, and the eyes move — they are alive in the dead face, scanning the dark in all directions. He holds it up sometimes to see further, using it as a lantern. In its light the road goes dark. He is also called the Gan Ceann, the Without Head, and he appears in Irish folklore as one of the most unambiguous death omens in the tradition — not a creature that might kill you, but a creature whose appearance means that someone specific is already fated to die.

He rides the roads and laneways of rural Ireland at night, and when he stops, he stops for a reason. He stops at the gate of a house, or at a crossroads, or at a field boundary, and he speaks one name. Not a sentence, not a warning, just a name. Whoever holds that name will be dead before the sun rises. There is no negotiation, no appeal, no protective measure. Gold is the only substance that affects him — if you throw a gold coin at him, or display gold jewelry, he will retreat, but what this means for the person whose name he has already spoken is unclear. He does not come back for the same name twice because there is no need.

His whip is the spine of a human corpse. The blood that splashes from a basin he sometimes carries, or from a wound that never heals in his side, will blind anyone it touches. Gates and barriers open before him of their own accord, which means that no locked door or walled estate provides protection. He passes through the distinctions that humans make between safe and unsafe, between inside and outside, because death makes no such distinction.

There are accounts of people trying to watch him from windows without being seen, and some of these accounts end with the observer being splashed with blood that ruins their sight permanently. Curiosity about death, Irish tradition suggests, is an invitation. The appropriate response to hearing hoofbeats on a country road at night — in the specific silence, the particular rhythm that is not quite right — is to put out any light you have and remain still and wait for the sound to pass. If it stops, do not go to the window. If your name is not called, the night has passed safely. If it is called, the window would not have made any difference.
