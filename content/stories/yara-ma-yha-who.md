It lives in fig trees. This is the central practical warning in the Aboriginal accounts of the Yara-ma-yha-who — do not rest or sleep under a fig tree, because the creature that waits in the branches above is not obvious, and it drops without warning. The Yara-ma-yha-who is small by the standards of most supernatural predators, approximately the height of a child, with red skin and a large head and a mouth that is wider than its face in a way that should not be anatomically possible. Its hands and feet have suckers on them, like an octopus.

The attack proceeds in stages. First: the creature drops from the tree onto its victim and attaches. The suckers drain blood, weakening the person rapidly without any visible wound. The Yara-ma-yha-who then swallows the victim whole — which is where the impossibly large mouth becomes relevant — and goes to sleep. When it wakes, it regurgitates the person. The person is alive. They are shorter than before. They are slightly redder. The next time it happens to the same person, they are shorter and redder again. Eventually, after enough cycles, the person has become a Yara-ma-yha-who themselves.

This mechanism — the gradual transformation of victim into predator through repeated encounters — is unusual in supernatural folklore and gives the creature a different quality from simple monsters. It is not just killing. It is converting. The person who survives a first encounter and is found and recovered by their family should be watched carefully thereafter, not because they are dangerous yet, but because they have been changed, and the change is progressive, and the person who fell asleep under a fig tree once is now drawn to fig trees in a way they were not before.

The appropriate response during the attack, if you find yourself in one, is reportedly to remain calm and cooperate. The Yara-ma-yha-who, when it encounters no resistance, tends to lose interest and wander away before the swallowing stage. This is easier to recommend than to practice when something is draining your blood from the inside of its hands, but the tradition suggests that panic is what completes the cycle and that stillness breaks it.
