It cannot see. This is the first thing to understand about the Viy, and also the most deceptive fact about it, because the blindness is temporary and the cure for it is worse than anything sight usually causes. The Viy is a creature from deep Ukrainian folklore, perhaps a king of the gnomes or of the earth spirits, perhaps something older and harder to categorize, and it is described as short and squat and covered in black earth like something that has been buried and dug up repeatedly over centuries. Its eyelids are so heavy — in some versions made of iron, in some versions simply grotesquely overgrown — that they rest on the ground. Attendant creatures must lift them.

When the eyelids are lifted, it sees. And when the Viy sees something, that thing is destroyed.

The story most associated with the Viy comes from Gogol, who claimed to be transcribing existing folk material, though how much he invented is disputed. A seminary student named Khoma is tricked into spending three nights reading prayers over the corpse of a witch who had ridden him like a horse through the sky. During the first two nights he protects himself by drawing a circle of chalk around himself and reading the prayers without looking up. The dead witch and her attendant spirits cannot cross the circle. On the third night the witch summons the Viy.

Khoma hears it moving toward him in the dark, something heavy and dragging, and he keeps reading, keeps his eyes down, and then a voice says: *Lift my eyelids. I cannot see.* And attendant spirits lift the iron eyelids. And the Viy looks directly at the chalk circle and sees through it — sees Khoma — and Khoma breaks the only rule that had been protecting him. He looks back. 

The first and only look is also the last. In the story Khoma dies immediately, the spirits fly shrieking in confusion through the church windows and stick there, frozen, and the church is abandoned. The moral is one that recurs throughout Slavic supernatural tradition: there are things you must not look at, must not turn toward, must not acknowledge, and the moment you do, the protection ends. The circle is maintained by attention, and attention has a breaking point. The Viy is what waits on the other side of that breaking point, patient in the dark with its eyelids dragging, waiting to be seen.
