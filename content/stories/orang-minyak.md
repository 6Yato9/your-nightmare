He is covered entirely in black oil or grease — every inch of exposed skin slicked, making him nearly invisible in the dark and impossible to hold. If someone grabs him, their hands slide off. He moves between them. He cannot be restrained by normal means. The Orang Minyak — the Oily Man — appears in Malaysian folklore as a human being who made a deal: supernatural invulnerability and invisibility in exchange for a specific number of women, delivered to whatever power granted the ability. The deal has a timeline. The Orang Minyak must complete his quota or the power is withdrawn, or worse. So he is not simply a predator. He is a predator under contract, operating with urgency.

The accounts from the 1950s and 1960s in Malaysia — when the Orang Minyak achieved its most concentrated period of news coverage and public panic — describe a figure moving through kampung neighborhoods in the night, entering homes through windows, leaving the oily residue of his passage on windowsills and door frames. Women would wake to find him standing over them. Some managed to escape. Others did not, and the crime was described using the language of supernatural attack rather than ordinary assault, with consequences for how seriously it was investigated by the colonial and post-colonial authorities of the period.

Feminist scholars reading the Orang Minyak tradition note its function in a specific social context: the figure of the Oily Man gave a name and a supernatural frame to the experience of nocturnal attack while simultaneously placing the perpetrator outside the community — he is not a neighbor, not someone's relative, but a being under demonic contract, set apart by the visible sign of his transgression. Whether this frame protected or obscured real perpetrators is a question the scholarship finds uncomfortable but necessary.

The protections against the Orang Minyak are spiritual: verses from the Quran, the presence of a virtuous person whose piety is a deterrent, the wearing of white during specific nights. The grease, in one account, can only be broken by someone who genuinely does not fear him. Whether anyone has ever not feared him is not specified.
