Along the coastal paths and marshlands of Norfolk, Suffolk, and Essex, there has been reported for centuries a black dog of impossible size — larger than any natural dog, as large as a calf or a small horse, running silently along the cliff paths and churchyard walls. Its eyes are described in every account as burning: a single red eye, or two eyes like hot coals, or a cyclopic lamp of sickly green fire depending on the telling. It runs without making sound. Its paws leave no prints in the soft coastal mud.

The name Black Shuck derives from the Old English scucca, meaning demon or devil, and the creature has been catalogued in the folklore of East Anglia since at least the sixteenth century. The most famous documented encounter occurred in 1577, when a massive black dog reportedly burst through the doors of St. Mary's Church in Bungay during a violent thunderstorm, ran through the congregation, killed two worshippers and left a third shriveled and contracted — and then crossed to Holy Trinity Church in Blythburgh seven miles away, leaving scorch marks on the door that locals called the Devil's Fingerprints and pointed out to visitors for centuries afterward. The scorch marks can still be seen.

To see Black Shuck is, in most traditions, an omen of death — either yours or someone close to you, within a year of the encounter. This is the dominant interpretation across the region, though some accounts complicate it: there are stories of Black Shuck accompanying travelers safely along dark roads and then vanishing when the traveler reached safety, a guardian reading that sits uncomfortably beside the death omen tradition. The creature may be both, depending on something the accounts do not specify.

Unlike many folkloric entities, Black Shuck has accumulated a remarkable body of 20th-century sightings, reported to local newspapers and paranormal investigators in entirely consistent terms — the size, the silent running, the burning eyes, the disappearance into solid walls or into the air — by people with no apparent connection to one another across decades. The A12 road corridor between Ipswich and Lowestoft has been particularly active. Truck drivers, cyclists, late-night walkers: different people, same dog.

The standard folk advice is straightforward: if you see it, do not follow it. Do not speak to it. Do not attempt to touch it. Acknowledge it silently and continue on your way. Some accounts add that iron horseshoes nailed above the door offered protection — not prevention, but protection. Black Shuck does not enter houses where iron is displayed. It watches from the road, from the churchyard gate, from the edge of the marsh, its eyes burning steadily in the dark.
