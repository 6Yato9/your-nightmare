The Kikimora does not come from outside. She is already in the house when you move in, or she arrives because of something you did, or something someone did before you, and once she is established in the walls she is very difficult to remove. In Slavic folklore — across Russia, Ukraine, Poland, and the surrounding regions — the Kikimora is a small female household spirit, roughly the size of a child but wizened and wrong-looking, with a long nose and a body like a bundle of sticks, and she lives in the dark spaces of the structure: behind the stove, under the floorboards, in the corners of the cellar.

What she does to a household is nothing dramatic. She does not kill. She does not appear dramatically. She specializes in the sustained, cumulative damage that looks like nothing in particular: the yarn that was neatly wound last night is tangled this morning. The children wake screaming from nightmares and cannot explain what they saw. The chickens stop laying. The goat produces sour milk. The husband finds himself unreasonably irritable. Sleep becomes light and unsatisfying throughout the house, and everyone feels vaguely that something is wrong, but nothing specific enough to point at.

A Kikimora arrives in a house built on cursed ground — a location where someone died badly and was not properly buried. She also arrives when a builder hides a small poppet — a doll made of rags or straw — inside the walls during construction, an act of malice against whoever will live there. This means the Kikimora is, in some sense, placed rather than wandering: she is summoned by negligence or by deliberate harm and then bound to the location. The household that discovers it has a Kikimora must first determine why.

The remedies are domestic in scale: washing all the household items before dawn with water steeped in specific herbs, reciting specific prayers, applying solutions of various kinds to the thresholds and corners where she lives. None of these is guaranteed. The effective method is to find the poppet, if one was placed, and remove it — but this means opening the walls. Many households simply learned to live with her, managing the symptoms, keeping the yarn in sealed containers, tolerating the restless sleep, negotiating a kind of truce with something inside the structure that had no intention of leaving.
