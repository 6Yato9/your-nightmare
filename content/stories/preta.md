The Preta is not a creature that hunts you. It is a state that you might become. In Buddhist cosmology, the realm of the hungry ghosts — the Preta realm — is one of the six realms of rebirth, and it is the destination of those who die with minds dominated by craving, greed, and insatiable desire. The transition to the Preta realm is not a punishment handed down by an external judge but a consequence of the mental state at death — the mind that dies wanting becomes, in its next form, a being whose defining characteristic is want without the capacity for satisfaction.

The traditional image of the Preta is specific and deliberately uncomfortable: a being with an enormous belly, representing the vastness of its hunger, and a mouth and throat so narrow that nothing can actually pass through. It reaches for food constantly. It can smell food, can perceive food, can want food with an intensity that has no parallel in human experience, and it cannot eat. Or it can eat, but whatever it eats turns to ash or fire in its mouth. Or the food it reaches for becomes repulsive the moment it is grasped. The suffering of the Preta is not the suffering of starvation but the suffering of infinite desire meeting infinite frustration, without the release of either satisfaction or death.

Pretas wander the edge of the human world. They are not malevolent in intent — they are not seeking to harm people — but their proximity can cause harm, a kind of spiritual pollution or misfortune that attaches to places where many Pretas gather: crossroads at night, places of waste and refuse, the sites of great suffering. Buddhist ritual practice addresses this directly. The ceremony of burning offerings — paper money, food, symbolic goods — sends these things to the hungry ghosts so that they can receive, even in their realm, something that alleviates the perpetual hunger slightly. The Ghost Festival in East Asian Buddhism is structured around this feeding, a ritual acknowledgment that the Preta realm exists alongside the human world and that the relationship between them benefits from attention.

The teaching purpose of the Preta image is clear: it shows what greed looks like from the inside, carried to its logical conclusion through the mechanism of rebirth. But the tradition does not reduce the Preta to a symbol. It is a real category of being, and the hungry ghosts that cluster near the edges of human life are understood to be genuinely there, genuinely suffering, and genuinely in need of what the living can provide.
