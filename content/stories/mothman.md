The first sighting was in November 1966, when two young couples driving near an abandoned TNT plant outside Point Pleasant, West Virginia, reported seeing a large grey figure with wings folded against its body and two enormous red eyes that reflected their headlights like fire. It paced their car at over a hundred miles an hour. They drove to the county courthouse and reported it, which is how the newspaper got hold of the story and gave it a name.

Over the next thirteen months, more than a hundred people in the Point Pleasant area reported seeing the same thing. A tall figure, grey or brown, with a wingspan wider than a man is tall, and those eyes — always the eyes, always red, always reflective, positioned not in a face exactly but somewhere near the top of the body where a face should be. It perched on rooftops. It followed cars. It was seen standing in fields and on the banks of the Ohio River. Several people reported that after encountering it, their televisions and radios malfunctioned for days.

There were also the men in black — strangers who called on witnesses shortly after their sightings, driving large dark cars with no license plates, asking questions in flat, affectless voices, warning people not to speak publicly about what they had seen. A journalist named John Keel, who came to investigate, reported receiving threatening phone calls and strange transmissions on his equipment. He became convinced that whatever was happening in Point Pleasant was not simply a matter of an unknown animal.

On December 15, 1967, the Silver Bridge connecting Point Pleasant to Ohio collapsed during rush hour. Forty-six people died. It was the worst infrastructure disaster in West Virginia history. Witnesses reported seeing the Mothman on the bridge in the moments before it fell.

After the collapse, the sightings stopped. Whether this means the entity departed, its purpose fulfilled, or that the grief of a community simply had no more room for such stories, is a question that remains open. What John Keel argued in his subsequent book was that the Mothman was not a creature in any biological sense but something older — an intelligence that appears before catastrophe, drawn to it or perhaps announcing it, whose warnings people see but cannot read in time. The eyes watching from the dark above a bridge that is about to fall. The wings folded at the edge of something that cannot be prevented.

Point Pleasant erected a steel statue of the Mothman in 2003. It stands in the town center, gleaming and human-shaped with its wings spread, and tourists photograph themselves beside it every year. Whether the thing that stood on the bridge that December night resembled the statue at all is unclear. Most of the original witnesses said it didn't look like anything they had words for.
