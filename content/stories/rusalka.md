In the forests and rivers of Russia, Ukraine, and Poland, there is a week in late spring — Rusalka Week, the seventh week after Easter — when the boundary between the living world and the world of the restless dead grows dangerously thin. During these days, the Rusalki emerge from their rivers and lakes and sit in the branches of trees near the water, combing their long wet hair, singing, and watching for the living who venture too close.

A Rusalka is the spirit of a woman who died an untimely, unnatural death — most often by drowning, whether by accident, by her own hand in grief over a faithless lover, or by murder. She is bound to the body of water where she died, and she is not at peace. The wrong that ended her life has become her substance. She is made of grief and cold and the particular anger of a person who was interrupted before they could finish being alive.

In her appearance she is described as beautiful — pale as water, hair the color of river weeds, eyes the color of deep pools where the light does not reach. She may be seen dancing in moonlit clearings near the water, and the sound of her dancing is said to be irresistible. Men who follow it find the dance moving inexorably toward the river's edge. The Rusalka does not push. She simply keeps dancing, keeps drawing forward, until the water closes over both of them.

Children were considered especially vulnerable during Rusalka Week. The traditional protections involved wearing wormwood and carrying iron, both of which the Rusalki feared. People avoided swimming, doing laundry near the riverbank, or being alone near water after dark. Specific days were forbidden for certain activities — planting, weaving, washing — because the Rusalki would punish those who worked during their festival time by tickling them to death, a particular form of their violence that appears across the folklore.

At the end of Rusalka Week, communities performed ceremonies to send the spirits back to their waters — effigies were burned, offerings left at the riverbank, rituals of release performed for women who had died badly and deserved a better rest than they were getting. The acknowledgment that they existed, that their deaths mattered, that they were still grieved — this was the only appeasement that sometimes worked.
