Somewhere in the rural Philippines, on a night when the moon is hidden and the dogs have gone quiet, a woman stands alone in a field and does something that should be impossible. Her upper body separates from her lower half at the torso. Enormous bat-like wings unfurl from her back, wet and dark. She rises into the sky, leaving her legs standing in the grass below — still upright, still rooted, waiting. She will need them again before dawn.

The Manananggal is an older and more specifically terrifying relative of the Aswang, distinguished by this singular act of bodily severance. The name comes from the Tagalog word tanggal, meaning to separate or remove. She is one of the most feared supernatural beings in Filipino folklore precisely because of the imagery: the self-divided creature, half of her airborne and predatory, half anchored and vulnerable, both halves necessary, neither half complete. It is a horror of incompleteness and wrongness that operates on a level below rational thought.

Her primary targets are pregnant women, whose unborn children she seeks through the roof or window of a house using her elongated tongue — a proboscis that can slip through the smallest gap to reach the sleeping mother within. She drains the fetus, or sometimes the blood of the woman herself. The family wakes in the morning to find the mother wasted and pale, and no obvious cause. The second and third visits are worse. By the time the community understands what is happening, the harm is often done.

The most well-known defense against the Manananggal is also her greatest vulnerability: her lower half, left standing in the grass, must be found and destroyed before dawn. Salt, ash, or crushed garlic rubbed into the severed waist prevents her from reattaching. If she cannot reattach before sunrise, she dies with the coming of the light — the upper half burning, the lower half collapsing. Hunting parties in rural Visayas were once organized specifically to search for the standing legs while the creature was abroad, and the garlic and salt were carried for exactly this purpose.

She can be identified in her human form, though identification is dangerous. She avoids the smell of garlic entirely. She does not cast a complete shadow. She has difficulty crossing thresholds that have been salted. And if you catch her in an unguarded moment near her hiding place at dawn, you may see the line at her waist — the seam where the halves reluctantly meet.
