In September 1837, a letter was read aloud at a public meeting convened by the Mayor of London. It had been sent, anonymously, by a nobleman who claimed that something unprecedented was terrorizing the residents of Barnes and Richmond in the south of the city. He had collected accounts from multiple witnesses. They described a figure that could leap over carriages and garden walls from a standing start, that breathed blue and white fire from its mouth, that had hands with cold metallic claws, and eyes described as red orbs. It had grabbed several women, torn at their clothing with its claws, and disappeared with a bound over the nearest obstacle.

The newspaper reports that followed produced more witnesses, and the phenomenon spread. Spring-Heeled Jack — the name given by the press — was seen not only in London but in the Midlands, in Liverpool, in the north of England, in Sheffield and Wolverhampton and Northampton, over a period spanning from 1837 to roughly 1904. The accounts shared consistent elements: the leaping ability, the fire, the red eyes, the metallic claws, the general impression of something wearing a tight-fitting oilskin costume with a strange helmet, moving with physical capability that no human being possessed.

The explanations proposed over the decades ranged from the practical to the extraordinary. A human prankster, perhaps a nobleman engaged in elaborate antisocial amusements. A gas-lit theatrical device on stilts. An escaped escaped experimental device. Collective hysteria amplified by press coverage. An actual supernatural entity, origin unspecified. No explanation has ever achieved general acceptance, partly because the consistency and geographic spread of the accounts over sixty-seven years makes the prankster theory increasingly strained, and partly because some of the encounters were reported by multiple simultaneous witnesses who gave independent accounts that matched.

The last confirmed sighting was in 1904, at a Liverpool street corner, where a crowd watched him leap continuously over a row of terraced houses for several minutes before disappearing. After that, nothing. Whatever it was — if it was one thing, if it was the same thing in London in 1837 and Liverpool in 1904 — it stopped appearing. No body was found. No equipment was recovered. Victorian England had produced its mystery and the mystery had politely concluded itself, leaving behind nothing but newspaper archives that no one has been able to fully explain.
