The Ponaturi come from beneath the sea. In Māori cosmology they are a class of supernatural beings — sometimes described as fair-skinned or pale, moving with an inhuman quality, numerous — who inhabit the deep water during the day and surface at night. The specific vulnerability that makes them both dangerous and ultimately defeatable is absolute: they cannot survive sunlight. Even the first hint of dawn will kill them, and they know this, which means they keep very careful track of the night. They must be back in the water before the sun comes up or they die. This precision about timing is, in the most famous story involving them, exactly what is exploited.

The story of Niwareka and Mataora, and separately the account of Tāwhaki and the Ponaturi, place these creatures in direct conflict with human heroes. In the Tāwhaki account, the Ponaturi have killed Tāwhaki's father and are holding his body in a great house where they sleep in numbers during the day. Tāwhaki and his brother want to retrieve the body and take revenge. They do this by manipulating the timing. His grandmother, who helps them, prevents the Ponaturi from knowing when dawn has come — she covers the cracks in the house through which they check the sky, delays the call of dawn birds, creates the impression that the night continues when it does not. The Ponaturi sleep past the point of safety. When the deception is finally revealed and they rush for the sea, the sun has already risen.

The Ponaturi thus represent a threat that is overcome not by strength or magic but by knowledge of what the enemy requires to survive, and a willingness to deny it. This is a different kind of hero narrative — not combat but patience and cunning, and specifically the cunning of an elderly woman whose knowledge of birds and timing is more useful than any weapon. The deep sea people, with all their supernatural capability, are undone by someone who understood their schedule better than they did.
