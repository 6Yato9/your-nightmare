The ice on an Arctic bay is not a floor. It is a lid, and what lives beneath it has been listening since before the first humans arrived on the tundra. The Inuit have known this for thousands of years, which is why children are taught from the moment they can walk that they must never go near the sea ice alone, must never run across a frozen bay without an adult present, and must immediately retreat if they hear the sound the Qallupilluit make — a sound like knocking, or like something tapping against ice from underneath.

The Qallupilluit are pale and thin and move through water the way water moves. They have long fingers designed for reaching through cracks, and they can sense the vibration of small feet on the ice above them. A child who steps too close to a pressure crack, who lingers at the edge, who does not hear the tapping until it is very close — that child is taken. The hand that emerges from the ice water is cold and impossibly strong and does not let go. What happens to the child after is not spoken of in detail. The stories stop at the moment of taking. This is deliberate. The silence is part of the warning.

In some Inuit communities, the Qallupilluit are described as caring for the children they take — raising them in underwater settlements, feeding them, keeping them. This is understood to be a horror of a different kind than simple drowning. The child is alive but unreachable, living in a world of cold and dark and silence beneath the ice, among creatures that are not human, and will never come home. The parents on the surface will stand at the crack for hours afterward. There is nothing to find.

The stories function as they were always intended to function: as instructions. The Arctic ice is genuinely dangerous, and a child who understood this in the abstract but not the specific would not treat it with sufficient caution. The Qallupilluit make the danger something with a face, or rather something without a face, which is worse. Something that wants you. Something that is patient. Something that has been down there under every frozen surface you have ever walked across, counting your steps, waiting for the moment you stop paying attention.

The knocking sound has been reported by adults as well. Those who hear it and do not see what made it tend not to go back to that stretch of ice. This is the correct response.
