What makes the Toyol unusual among the supernatural beings of Malay and Singaporean folklore is that it is not encountered accidentally. No one stumbles upon a Toyol. It is acquired — purchased from a *bomoh*, a practitioner of traditional Malay folk magic, in a deliberate transaction. The buyer wants something: money, advantage over a competitor, goods that would otherwise be unattainable. The bomoh provides a small jar that contains the spirit of a stillborn or aborted infant, bound through ritual into service. The buyer takes the jar home and takes on the obligations.

The Toyol, when fed, goes out at night and steals. It enters houses, moves small objects, takes coins and jewelry and food, and returns before dawn. Its owner prospers modestly and unnaturally and neighbors begin to wonder why their own possessions keep disappearing. But the Toyol is not simply a tool — it is a child spirit, which means it is petulant and demanding and will fail in its work, or return it badly, if it feels neglected. The traditional feeding is blood from the owner's thumb, sucked from the tip like a child nursing, and the spirit requires this intimacy or it sulks. As it grows more powerful and more attached to its owner, its hunger grows. The thumb blood stops being sufficient. The requests escalate.

The bindings that keep a Toyol subordinate are maintained through attention and feeding and the ritual care of the jar. If an owner dies without passing the Toyol to another person through the proper ceremony, the spirit is released from its binding and becomes something else — a free-floating infant spirit, crying in the dark around the house where it was kept, sometimes seen sitting in the branches of trees near the property, sometimes visible as a small dark-eyed child in rooms it should not be in. The neighbors, who knew there was something off about that household, now have more specific information.

The purchase of a Toyol is understood, within the tradition, to be an act with permanent moral consequences. The wealth it brings is called dirty wealth, and it is believed to carry misfortune for the owner's children and grandchildren. The bomoh who sells it does not warn buyers of this, which is its own kind of transaction. The thing in the jar is patient. It does not need to explain its long-term costs up front.
