In Islamic cosmology, God created three kinds of sentient beings: angels from light, humans from clay, and jinn from smokeless fire. Within the hierarchy of the jinn, the Ifrit occupy the highest tier of power, and almost the lowest tier of compliance. They are intelligent and willful and ancient, and they do not forget injuries or obligations, and both of these qualities make them extraordinarily dangerous to deal with. The Arabic root of the word suggests corruption, or cunning, or rebellion — the Ifrit are the jinn who said no.

They appear in the Quran obliquely and in *One Thousand and One Nights* extensively, and in both sources the picture is similar: a being of enormous capability and great pride who can be compelled but not truly controlled. In Sulaiman ibn Dawud — the Solomon of Islamic tradition — they appear as servants bound to great works. An Ifrit offers to bring the throne of Bilqis from the distant south before a man could stand from his seat. This is the Ifrit's understanding of its own power: what would take humans months, it offers to do between heartbeats.

The binding of an Ifrit requires a sorcerer of considerable ability and a seal of power — in tradition, a ring inscribed with the seal of Solomon could command any jinn. But the binding is a negotiation, not a conquest. An Ifrit that has been bound carries its resentment as carefully as it carries out its task, and it looks for the exact boundary of its instructions, the precise edge of what it was told, so that it can fulfill the letter of the command in a way that destroys the spirit of it. The sorcerer who says *bring me gold* and means *make me wealthy* may find that the Ifrit has brought gold that belongs to a king who will now hunt the sorcerer to his death.

When an Ifrit is not bound, it is essentially free to act on its nature, which is to say on its pride, its old grievances, and its sense of what the world owes it. It can travel vast distances in moments, assume many forms, manipulate fire, and move between the physical world and the unseen world at will. It lives in desolate places — ruins, remote deserts, the sites of disasters. People who walk through these places at dusk without taking precautions — without speaking the proper words, without salt, without iron — may attract the attention of something very old and very patient that has been waiting in the ruin for a long time, and that has its own ideas about what the encounter should cost.
