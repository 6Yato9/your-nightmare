When a storm comes in from the west over the Great Plains, the old knowledge says to pay attention to what it looks like at the leading edge. A natural storm has a certain quality of sky — the particular green-grey of hail coming, the flat anvil shape of a supercell, the way the light goes before rain. A Thunderbird is different. The darkness at the front of a Thunderbird storm has depth to it, and the lightning is not random but directed, and the thunder is not an echo but something that precedes what makes it. The Thunderbird strikes and the sound arrives before any of it makes physical sense.

Indigenous nations across a vast stretch of North America — from the Pacific Northwest to the Great Lakes to the Gulf Coast — carry knowledge of the Thunderbird, and the specific details vary widely by nation and region. In some traditions it is a single being; in others, a species or a family. In some, it is among the oldest of created things; in others, it was always here, part of the structure of the world rather than a resident of it. What is consistent is scale — the Thunderbird is immense. Its wingspan is measured in the distance that clouds cover. When it blinks, lightning happens. When it beats its wings, the sound fills valleys. The rain that comes with a thunderstorm is the water that drips from its feathers.

The Thunderbird and the Underwater Panther, the great horned serpent — Mishibizhiw in Anishinaabe tradition — are ancient enemies. Their conflict is the conflict between the sky and the deep water, between the above and the below, and it is ongoing. A lake disturbed by an unusual storm, waves where there should be no waves, is sometimes understood as the result of the Thunderbird striking at something in the water below. The collision of these two powers is what storms are. The human world is situated between them.

Several traditions maintain that the Thunderbird can take human form, or that it has at times chosen to interact with specific people, usually those who have proven themselves through great endurance or suffering. These encounters are among the most sacred narratives in the traditions that hold them, and most of their content is not for general circulation. What is shareable is the frame: the Thunderbird sees what happens on the ground below because it is always above it, and what it chooses to do about what it sees is its own judgment, not available to human petition. It will act according to its own understanding of what the world requires. That understanding is very old.
