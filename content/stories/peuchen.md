The air in the Chilean countryside, usually crisp with the scent of pine and damp earth, grew unnaturally still as dusk bled into the deep violet of night. A chill, sharper than any mountain breeze, snaked through the valleys, carrying with it a faint, leathery musk that clung to the throat. This was the harbinger, the scent that made a seasoned campesino quickly bar the door and draw the curtains tight, not daring to glance at the moonlit sky. For in those moments of deepening twilight, when the last rays of sun slipped behind the jagged peaks of the Andes, the Peuchen was said to awaken, a predatory shadow unfurling its immense wings from some hidden crag.

From the ancient Mapuche traditions, whispered around flickering hearths for generations, comes the tale of the Peuchen, a creature of chilling duality and insatiable hunger. It is described most often as a giant serpent, sometimes with the leathery wings of a colossal bat, its scales shimmering with an unholy sheen in the scarce light. Unlike the straightforward predators of the natural world, the Peuchen does not rely on fang or claw in its initial assault. Instead, its most terrifying weapon is its gaze—a hypnotic stare that paralyzes its chosen victim, freezing them in a state of conscious terror, unable to move, unable to scream, as the monstrous entity draws closer.

Once its victim is ensnared by its petrifying gaze, the Peuchen descends. Its true horror then reveals itself, for this creature is a sanguinary beast, a blood-drinker of the most complete and devastating kind. It drains its victims entirely, leaving behind nothing but a hollow husk, a withered shell of what was once a living being. The stories tell of unfortunate travelers found slumped on lonely paths, or even livestock in remote corrals, utterly devoid of blood, their bodies eerily light, as if gravity itself had lost its hold on them. The silence of such discoveries, the utter absence of struggle or sound, is perhaps the most unsettling detail, a testament to the creature's silent, paralyzing efficiency.

The folklore of the Peuchen is deeply intertwined with the rural life of Chile, particularly in the south-central regions where Mapuche culture remains vibrant. It is a creature often associated with sorcerers or *brujos*, who are said to control or even transform into the Peuchen to exact their dark will. To protect oneself from this aerial vampiric horror, traditional remedies and practices are invoked. Carrying a thorn branch, or keeping a fire brightly burning through the night are common deterrents, as the Peuchen is said to dislike sharp objects and bright, consuming flames. Yet, the most potent defense, as with many such creatures of the night, lies in awareness—knowing the signs, respecting the stories, and never, ever, looking directly into the night sky when the strange, leathery scent of the Peuchen drifts on the wind. For to meet its gaze, even for a fleeting moment, is to invite an emptiness that transcends death itself.
