Late at night on a country road in Guatemala or El Salvador or Honduras, a lone traveler — and the tradition is specific that the traveler is almost always drunk, almost always a man, almost always someone who has stayed too long at a cantina and is now navigating home by the feel of the road under his feet — will sometimes become aware of a dog walking behind him. Not following, exactly, but keeping pace. A large dog, large enough that the sound of its claws on the packed earth is audible even over the traveler's own footsteps.

The question that matters, and that the traveler may not be sober enough to determine correctly, is what color the dog is.

The white Cadejo is a guardian. It accompanies the wandering drunk to keep him safe from the dangers of the night road — the ditch he might fall into, the robbery he might walk into, the other supernatural things that hunt at that hour. It is enormous and its eyes are red but its intention is protective. It will stay until the traveler reaches his door and will not be thanked. The black Cadejo is the opposite: it accompanies travelers to bring them to harm, to lead them off the road, to drive them mad, to deliver them to something worse. Its eyes are also red. In the dark, the colors can be confused.

The tradition in some Central American communities describes the two Cadejos as a single entity with two aspects, or as brothers, or as manifestations of the eternal opposition between protection and destruction that structures all supernatural forces. Others insist they are simply two different animals that have nothing to do with each other except that they occupy the same ecological niche of the night road. The practical upshot is the same: if you are walking alone at night and a very large dog falls into step behind you, you should stop and look at it carefully before you decide what to do, and you should be very sure of your answer before you proceed.

The smell is one indicator — the white Cadejo smells of wild herbs and open fields; the black Cadejo smells of sulfur. This is useful information if you can smell anything over the alcohol. Most travelers who have reported encounters with the Cadejo note that they understood which one it was only in retrospect.
