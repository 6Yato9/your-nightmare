The Jiangshi — literally "stiff corpse" — is one of China's most distinctive supernatural creatures, shaped by a specific cultural anxiety about the proper treatment of the dead. When a body is not buried promptly and correctly, when someone dies far from their ancestral home and their corpse cannot be transported back in time, when the hun soul separates from the po soul in a manner that leaves both unmoored — the body may reanimate. Rigor mortis has set in. It cannot bend its knees. It moves by hopping, arms extended forward for balance, a posture so specific and strange it has become iconic.

The creature is in a state of energetic starvation. It absorbs qi — life force — from the living, which in earlier accounts manifested as a kind of vampiric breath-stealing: the Jiangshi would hover over sleeping victims and inhale their qi directly. Later traditions added blood-drinking, influenced perhaps by the cross-contamination of vampire lore through trade routes. The creature's skin turns greenish or pale from the prolonged state of death; in some descriptions it is covered in white fur, a detail that lends it an additional layer of wrongness.

The folklore includes an unusually specific set of countermeasures that feel almost puzzle-like in their particularity. A Jiangshi navigates primarily by scent — it cannot see well and relies on detecting the breath of the living. If you hold your breath completely, it cannot find you; it will hop past in the dark, arms extended, unable to locate you inches away. A yellow paper talisman written with specific characters and pressed to its forehead will freeze it in place. Bags of sticky rice confuse it, as will a line of rice across a doorway — it must count every grain before crossing, and dawn may come before it finishes.

The Taoist priest — the Daoshi — was the traditional specialist in Jiangshi suppression. Armed with peachwood swords, mirrors, and the appropriate talismans, they could neutralize a risen corpse, contain it, and ensure it was properly reburied with the correct ceremonies to prevent recurrence. The mirror was particularly significant: when a Jiangshi saw its own reflection, it was confronted with the reality of what it was — dead, already gone — and some accounts say this alone was enough to break the animation.

The Jiangshi myth reflects a profound Chinese cultural preoccupation: the obligation to bury the dead properly, to return bodies to their home soil, to maintain the correct relationship between the living and those who have gone before. A Jiangshi is not evil so much as a consequence of neglect — the result of a ritual debt that was not paid. It exists as a reproach.
