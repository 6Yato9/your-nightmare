The Vetala inhabits the space between death and cremation, which in Hindu understanding is a threshold of particular danger — the body has died but the fires have not yet released the spirit, and this liminal body, hanging in a cremation ground or newly interred, is a site that certain entities can occupy. The Vetala is one of these entities, and the body it takes is changed by its presence: the hands and feet point backward, the face becomes grey and swollen, and the eyes, which should be closed, are open and alert and moving with an intelligence that the dead should not have.

It cannot be easily destroyed while it inhabits a body, and it can move between bodies if threatened — leaving one corpse and entering another nearby, which in a cremation ground where multiple bodies may be present creates an obvious strategic problem. It has existed long enough to have accumulated vast knowledge, which is what makes it genuinely dangerous in a way distinct from its physical presence. A creature that has been living in cremation grounds for centuries, moving between the bodies of people from every class and occupation, has absorbed the complete range of human experience, including the experience of death, and this makes it perceptive in ways that living people cannot easily anticipate.

The most famous Vetala appears in the Sanskrit collection *Baital Pachisi*, the Twenty-Five Tales of the Vampire, in which the king Vikramaditya is tasked by a sorcerer with carrying a Vetala-inhabited corpse from its hanging place to the sorcerer without speaking. Each time Vikramaditya lifts the body, the Vetala tells him a puzzle story that is so cleverly constructed that Vikramaditya inevitably speaks to answer it, at which point the Vetala flies back to its tree and the king must begin again. This repeats twenty-five times. The Vetala is testing the king's wisdom and also, in its way, trying to warn him about the sorcerer's true intentions.

What the tradition says about dealing with a Vetala in the real world is more practical: cremation ground and cemetery ground in Hindu practice is treated with serious caution after dark, and the proper rituals around cremation are understood to close the window of vulnerability. A body cremated with the right ceremonies in the right time provides no purchase for a Vetala. It is the neglected body, the improperly handled dead, the unburied or uncremated, that creates the opportunity. This is not simply superstition — it is a framework for treating the dead with the seriousness they deserve.
