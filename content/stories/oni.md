They are large. This is the first thing the tradition establishes about them — the Oni of Japanese folklore are massive, hulking figures that stand a head and shoulders above the largest human, with skin that is red or blue or black, with horns growing from their skulls, with the tusks of wild boars and the loincloth of a tiger and a tetsubo, an iron club studded with metal knobs, swung in one hand like a toy. They are the creatures stationed at the gates of Jigoku, the realm of the dead, where they serve the judgment of Emma-O, the king of hell. Sinners arrive before Emma-O and their lives are weighed, and the Oni administer what follows.

This is their formal function. Their informal relationship with the world of the living is more complex and more frightening. The Oni descend from the underworld at specific times — on the night of Setsubun, the day before the traditional start of spring, they walk the world and must be repelled by roasting soybean and throwing them at the doors and windows of houses while chanting *Oni wa soto, fuku wa uchi* — demons out, fortune in. The beans are understood to be caustic to them. This ritual has been performed for more than a thousand years, which suggests that the belief in the Oni's visits was taken seriously enough to maintain the defense indefinitely.

In Japanese Buddhist thought, the Oni carry strong symbolic weight: they are the consequence of human sin, born from people who committed great wickedness and became something monstrous in death, or from the accumulated evil of the world taking physical form. They punish, but they were once human in some accounts, which gives them a different quality from the demons of traditions where evil is simply external and foreign. The Oni knows what it is like to be a person who made bad decisions. This does not make it compassionate. It makes it thorough.

There is also a tradition of the Oni that can be converted, persuaded, or enlisted. Several famous warrior legends involve heroes defeating an Oni not by destroying it but by impressing it, and the reformed Oni then becomes a ferociously loyal companion, its enormous strength redirected. The tetsubo that was a weapon becomes a useful tool. This is the ambivalence at the heart of many Oni stories: something terrible and powerful, if it can be met with sufficient courage or cleverness, can be made into something else. The danger is all in the meeting.
