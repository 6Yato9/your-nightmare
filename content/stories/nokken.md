The music comes from the water. On a still evening over a Norwegian or Swedish lake, a sound rises from the surface — fiddle music of extraordinary beauty, more technically accomplished than any human player, with something in the ornamentation that human fingers do not quite achieve. This is the first warning, and it is constructed to not feel like a warning. The Nøkken plays because it is bored, or because music is how it hunts, or because the music is simply what it is — the aesthetic expression of a being that has lived under the surface of cold northern lakes since before the first people arrived. The scholarship cannot establish motive. The tradition does not require motive. The music draws people to the water's edge, and people at the water's edge are in danger.

The Nøkken is a shapeshifter of considerable range. It appears as a white horse grazing at the shoreline — beautiful, apparently tame, which is a recurring theme in northern water spirit folklore and which should by now have been recognized as a pattern. It appears as a handsome young man sitting on a rock, wringing water from his hair. It appears as an older man with a fiddle, willing to teach. Several Scandinavian fiddle traditions trace their most distinctive techniques to encounters with the Nøkken — a musician who could teach the student to play in ways no human teacher knew, in exchange for something the student was not always aware they had agreed to give.

The underwater realm the Nøkken inhabits is understood in Norwegian tradition as a complete and functioning world. It has its own society. The Nøkken is not an isolated predator but something more like a sovereign of its domain, which makes interactions with it negotiations between worlds rather than simple attacks. A person who treats the Nøkken with appropriate respect — who acknowledges its domain and its power before entering the water or walking the shoreline — is in a different relationship to it than a person who wades in ignorantly.

Offerings of tobacco, or simply the spoken acknowledgment of its name, have traditionally been held to provide some protection on a dark lake. The fiddle music continuing without a visible source is information: the Nøkken is present, and it is choosing how to present itself. The shape it chooses tells you something about what it intends to do with you. The horse is the most dangerous shape. Do not go near the horse.
