In the Slavic tradition the dragon is not a Western beast. It does not simply hoard gold and wait to be slain by a saint or a knight. The Zmey — also spelled Zmij, Zmij-Gorynych in Russian tales — is a creature of active and ongoing menace to the human world, a being that demands tribute, destroys harvests, and takes people as though they are owed to it. But it is also a being of the air and fire that can take human form, and this ambiguity — predator and seducer, destroyer and negotiating partner — runs through every account of it.

The appearance in its full form is familiar in shape but not in scale: three heads in the most common telling, though the number varies from one to twelve, each head breathing fire, the body massive and scaled and taloned. It lives in caves and mountains and the deep forest, and when it comes out it leaves scorched earth. The droughts of summer in folk belief were sometimes attributed to the Zmey drinking the rivers and the rain — consuming water because it can, because that is its nature, because the dry season is simply what happens when a dragon is present and no one has made the correct arrangements to limit it.

The arrangements were real. Communities in Russian and Ukrainian tradition held specific ceremonies in spring and autumn that functioned, in folk understanding, as negotiations — not prayers to be granted or ignored, but contractual interactions with a being that had specific needs and specific limits. Give the Zmey what it wanted in the right form at the right time and the harvest was protected. Fail to do this and the failure had consequences that were understood as the dragon's response rather than random bad fortune.

The human form is where the Zmey becomes something different. In this form — a tall, handsome man, often with features that are slightly too regular, slightly too still — it comes to women and seduces them. The women waste away. In some tales they fall in love with something they do not recognize as a dragon. In others they know exactly what it is and continue the relationship anyway, which the tradition judges with more complexity than simple condemnation. The Zmey in human form is compelling in ways that the stories do not pretend to dismiss. It is only the ending that is constant.
