The legend begins with a patricide. A young man from the Venezuelan llanos — the great cattle plains — killed his father over a dispute so petty that different versions of the story cannot agree on what it was. Some say it was over the meat from a hunt. Some say it was over a woman. In all versions, the son disemboweled his father, and his grandfather, who was a man of severe and particular justice, punished him: the grandfather had the son whipped until the salt from the wounds would reach the bone, then released a pack of hounds to chase him, and cursed him to wander the llanos forever, carrying in a sack the bones of everyone he killed.

He is impossibly tall now — descriptions put him anywhere between two and five meters — and thin, all skeleton under the clothes of a llanero, a wide hat pulled low. He wanders across the plains at night, and he whistles. The whistle is his warning and his trick simultaneously. It is a musical phrase, do-re-mi-fa-sol-la-si, high and clear and audible for miles. Here is what everyone who lives on the llanos learns early: if the whistle sounds far away, El Silbón is close. If it sounds close, he has already passed you and you are safe. The distance of the sound is reversed. A whistling that seems to come from just over there, just beyond the next stand of trees, means he is very, very far away. A whistling that fills the air all around you, that seems to come from directly above you, means he is somewhere else entirely. This inversion is the trap. A person who hears a distant whistle and relaxes is the person who has miscalculated.

He targets drunk men and unfaithful husbands above all others, which connects him to the morality of the communities that tell his story, but he is not reliably selective. He has been seen following travelers on unmarked roads and sitting outside the windows of houses where children sleep. When he finds a victim, he first counts the bones in his sack — a dry, clicking percussion in the dark — and then acts. What he does is not described in detail, except that the victim is found in the morning cold and drained of bodily fluid, as though squeezed dry.

The protections against El Silbón are specific and practical: dogs howl when he approaches and their noise drives him off. A bullwhip cracked near him recalls the grandfather's punishment and frightens him away. Chili peppers hung near a door confuse him. None of these protections help a man alone on the plains at night who hears, very softly in the distance, the beginning of a melody. That man needs to understand the inversion. That man needs to understand that the sound coming to him from far away is the sound of something that is already there.
