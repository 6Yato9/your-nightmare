When lightning strikes a tree or a house in the Eastern Cape, and the sky is clear, a traditional healer may be called to examine the strike site before it is disturbed. What they are looking for is evidence of the Impundulu — the lightning bird — whose strike looks like weather but isn't. The Impundulu is a large supernatural bird with black and white plumage that crackles with electricity, capable of summoning lightning and directing it at a target. It is not wild. It is sent.

Among the Xhosa and Zulu peoples, the Impundulu is a familiar — a bound supernatural creature that belongs to a witch, specifically a female witch, and that is inherited by her daughters. The inheritance is not optional. When a woman who possesses an Impundulu dies, the bird passes to her daughter, and the daughter becomes responsible for feeding it. What it eats is blood — human blood, preferably from a young man, drawn through a kind of parasitic attachment. A man who has attracted the Impundulu's feeding will grow weak and thin over months, feverish and short of breath, the life being drawn out of him slowly. This wasting sickness, which looks like tuberculosis, is known in some traditional medical contexts as *isidliso* — something that is eating you from inside.

The witch who possesses the Impundulu can direct it to attack specific targets, using the lightning capability for more direct harm, or deploy it as a vehicle for the blood-drinking feeding against whoever she chooses. She can also take it as a lover, which is a detail that appears in the folklore and which sits at the intersection of supernatural horror and the tradition's complex understanding of female power and transgression.

The *isangoma* — traditional healer and diviner — is the appropriate counter-specialist. An isangoma can identify when an illness is the result of an Impundulu attack, can potentially identify the witch responsible, and can perform the rituals that sever the bird's connection to its victim. What cannot be done by any means currently available to the tradition is to destroy an Impundulu outright. The bird is too old and too bound to its lineage to be destroyed by any single practitioner. It can be expelled from a person. It can be redirected. It cannot be ended.
