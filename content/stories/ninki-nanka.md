Among the most consistent and specific prohibitions in West African river folklore is this: you must not look directly at the Ninki Nanka. Not because it will attack you immediately. Because seeing it kills you. Not at once — the death follows over the days after the encounter, a rapid wasting illness that admits no treatment, that moves faster than any known disease, and that claims everyone who claims to have seen the creature clearly. Those who survive encounters describe having glimpsed something enormous in the water and looked away in time, and even these partial sightings are understood to carry risk.

The description of the Ninki Nanka, assembled from accounts given by people who claim to have seen parts of it in the rivers and swamps of the Gambia, Guinea, Guinea-Bissau, and Senegal, is of something serpentine but vast — with a body like a crocodile but much larger, a neck that extends from the water like a column, a horse-like head, and reported scales that reflect light differently from any known animal. The river systems it inhabits are real and deep and not fully surveyed. Local communities have treated it with serious caution for as long as oral history extends.

In 2006, a team from the Centre for Fortean Zoology traveled to the Gambia specifically to investigate reports of the Ninki Nanka. They did not find the creature. They collected numerous eyewitness accounts, including from a man who claimed to have seen it directly as a child and who attributed his subsequent long illness — from which he recovered, unusually — to the encounter. The team noted that the consistency of the accounts across unconnected witnesses was difficult to dismiss entirely, even absent physical evidence.

The local response to questions about the Ninki Nanka is not the response of people relating folklore for entertainment. It is the response of people discussing something that continues to be actively dangerous. They know where it has been seen recently. They know which stretches of river to avoid and at what times of year. They are not interested in whether it corresponds to any known species. They are interested in not dying, and the method they have found effective for not dying is a combination of specific avoidances that the tradition has validated over generations. The avoidances work. Whatever is in the water is still there.
