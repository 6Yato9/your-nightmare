Among the five classes of jinn recognized in Islamic tradition and pre-Islamic Arabian folklore, the Marid occupy a position of particular power and particular danger. They are the oldest, the strongest, and the most autonomous. Where an Ifrit is fire and pride, the Marid is water and depth — vast, cold, and patient in a way that fire is not. They inhabit the deep seas and large bodies of water, and they have been there longer than the human world as it currently exists can remember.

They appear in the literature of the Arabian Nights as the djinn of unlimited capability — the ones who can actually deliver on the promise of any wish, who can transport a palace from one continent to another between sunset and sunrise, who can produce treasure that does not vanish at dawn. Their capability is not in question. What is in question is whether accessing that capability is worth what it costs in the approach.

The Marid are sovereign. They are bound by the laws that govern all jinn — they can be compelled by the proper seals and invocations — but they resist compulsion with a depth of resentment that the lesser djinn do not possess, and they have longer memories. A Marid that has been bound and used and released will remember the one who bound it for centuries. What it does with that memory, centuries later, when the original sorcerer is long dead and their descendants go innocently about their lives, is one of the darker threads of djinn lore.

The stories in which a Marid grants a wish freely — without coercion, because it chose to — are almost uniformly positive. A sailor rescued from drowning who treated the sea with reverence and received in return an inexplicable survival, a calm where there should have been storm. These encounters are understood as gifts, and they are distinguished from the contracted gifts of bound djinn by exactly this quality: they were given because the Marid wanted to give them. What the Marid wants is rarely available for human purchase. The sea does not negotiate with people, except on very unusual occasions, and when it does the terms are always the sea's own.
