A fox that lives long enough becomes something else. A hundred years is the first threshold — at a hundred years, a fox can take human form. At five hundred years, it develops a second tail. At a thousand years, nine tails have grown, the fur has turned white or gold, and the creature has become what the Chinese tradition calls a *huli jing*, a fox spirit of the highest order, capable of deceiving anyone, immune to most spiritual countermeasures, and old enough that its understanding of human desire is both encyclopedic and clinical.

They appear primarily as women — beautiful, slightly too perfect, with an attention to detail that is more observant than warm. They attach themselves to men of wealth or learning, become wives or concubines, and over the course of months the men begin to fail. The traditional explanation is that the huli jing drains *jing*, the vital essence or life force, through intimate proximity. The man grows pale and tired and thin and eventually dies. The huli jing moves on. In stories told across the Tang and Song dynasties, this pattern repeats endlessly: a scholar sees a beautiful woman at the gate of a deserted mansion; she is gracious and learned and captivating; his health declines; an elderly monk or a Taoist priest recognizes what she is and intervenes or fails to intervene, depending on the lesson the story is trying to teach.

What the tradition is more ambivalent about is whether the huli jing is doing something evil or simply something natural. They are predators of a kind, but they are often described as lonely, capable of genuine attachment within the terms of what they are, aware of their own nature with a clarity that is almost poignant. Some of the oldest and most powerful nine-tailed fox spirits in Chinese literature are presented as morally complex figures — not monsters but something more uncomfortable, creatures who have lived long enough to understand humans better than humans understand themselves, and who use that understanding for survival because they have no choice.

The practical identification of a huli jing is done with fire — a fox spirit's shadow, revealed by specific kinds of flame, shows a fox, regardless of what human shape it has assembled. Some accounts say mirrors show it truly as well. But the most reliable identification is the slowness of the decline in someone close to you, the paleness that comes on gradually, the way the person you are worried about insists that nothing is wrong and that their companion is wonderful and that they have never been happier. That insistence, the peculiar certainty of it, is itself a sign.
