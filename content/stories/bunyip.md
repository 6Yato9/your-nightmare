No two descriptions of the Bunyip agree, and this is not because the witnesses are unreliable. It is because the Bunyip is not one thing seen by many people but many things, known by many peoples, across a continent where the inland waterways stretch for thousands of kilometers and the creatures that live in them have never all been catalogued. Aboriginal Australians across the continent — nations that speak different languages, live in different ecosystems, have different ceremonial traditions — all have a name or a concept for something large and dangerous that lives in still water: in billabongs, waterholes, rivers, lakes. The European settlers who arrived in the nineteenth century heard about it from multiple sources and applied one name, *bunyip*, which is from the Wemba-Wemba and Wergaia languages of Victoria and which roughly translates as devil or spirit.

What the traditional knowledge systems of various Aboriginal nations say about it varies enormously. It can be a massive hairy creature like a seal with the head of a dog. It can be a long-necked aquatic thing, half cow and half serpent. It can be smaller, more furtive, covered in feathers or scales or fur depending on who is describing it. The consistency is not in appearance but in behavior: it lives in water, it is dangerous, and people — especially women and children — should not go to the water alone after dark or before dawn, and should not be careless about the edges of billabongs.

In the colonial period, the settlers took the Bunyip seriously enough to look for it. Several nineteenth century newspapers published letters and articles proposing candidates — a surviving megafaunal animal, a species of large seal that had moved inland, an animal science had not yet classified. Bones were found and debated; the possibility of a real biological creature was discussed with genuine interest. The bones were eventually identified as belonging to known species and the formal scientific interest faded, but the creature did not disappear from the consciousness of communities living near the waterways of inland Australia.

What the Aboriginal traditions emphasize, beyond the animal's danger, is that the billabong itself is sacred — a site of ceremony, a place that holds significance across many generations, a location where the boundary between the mundane world and the deeper structure of the country is thin. The Bunyip is connected to this thinness. It is not simply a large predator. It is a guardian of something, or a manifestation of something, or a warning that the water has a relationship with the land that requires human respect. The appropriate response to the Bunyip, then, is not simply to avoid it but to approach the waterway correctly, with the right permission and the right understanding of what you are entering.
