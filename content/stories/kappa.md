It is roughly the size of a child, and it smells of fish, and the dish-shaped depression on top of its head holds water. The water must remain full. This is the Kappa's fatal constraint, its vulnerability embedded in its anatomy, and the Japanese tradition is very specific about it: if the water in the dish spills, the Kappa loses its supernatural strength and can be immobilized or even killed. Meeting a Kappa, therefore, should begin with a deep bow — not because politeness is warranted but because the Kappa's own politeness compels it to bow back, and when it bows, the water spills.

This detail — the courtesy that becomes a weapon — encodes something important about how the tradition thinks about the Kappa. It is dangerous but it is also rule-bound. It will keep promises. It can be made to swear oaths, and it honors them. Several Japanese legends turn on exactly this mechanism: a Kappa is caught at a disadvantage, it swears in exchange for its freedom to never harm the family that caught it, and it keeps this oath for generations, sometimes even helping the family in return. The Kappa that has made a binding promise is not a predator. It is almost a neighbor.

In its natural state, before any such arrangement, it pulls people — especially children — into rivers and drowns them. It removes the shirikodama, a mythical ball of spiritual essence located in the anus, which is the specific and anatomically peculiar detail that appears in virtually all Kappa accounts and which the tradition treats with complete seriousness. Sumo wrestlers were believed to have learned their art from Kappa, and the tradition of bowing before a match is sometimes traced to the same courtly vulnerability. The Kappa is the dangerous thing that can be humanized by formal structure — by ceremony, by oath, by the acknowledgment of mutual rules.

They live in rivers and ponds throughout Japan. In summer, when river drownings increase, children are warned. Several towns have erected warning signs near waterways featuring the Kappa's image. The warning signs do not explain the shirikodama.
