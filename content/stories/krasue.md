There is no way to prepare for encountering a Krasue in the dark. The image is one that the mind resists: a woman's head, beautiful, with long dark hair, floating at approximately eye height with no body attached, and hanging beneath it the wet luminous coil of its own entrails — organs that should not be visible, that should be interior, trailing loose and glowing faintly green in the dark. It moves. It hunts. It is looking for something.

The Krasue exists in Thai, Khmer, Lao, and Malaysian folklore under various names — it is the Krasue in Thailand, the Ap in Cambodia, the Phi Krasue everywhere in between — and the consensus of the tradition is that it was once a woman of high status. The curse that created her varies: in some accounts she was a princess who made a pact and the ritual went wrong; in others she was a noblewoman whose execution was botched; in others she was a powerful sorceress whose magic eventually consumed her physical form except for the head and its trailing viscera. What all accounts agree on is that by day she is a normal woman — she has a body during daylight hours, a life, perhaps a family, neighbors who know her — and by night the head separates from the sleeping body and goes out to feed.

She feeds on blood, on raw flesh, on the unborn. She clusters near animals about to give birth. She appears at the edges of villages near slaughterhouses and outdoor kitchens where the smell of blood reaches the dark. The entrails that trail below her serve a sensory function in some accounts — she can taste through them what is nearby, can navigate in the dark toward sources of what she needs. She returns before dawn and reattaches to her sleeping body, and the body wakes and goes about its day.

The body without the head is vulnerable. If it can be found and destroyed before the head returns, the Krasue dies. This is known. It creates, in the communities where the belief is strong, a particular vigilance about sleeping women — who is walking in the dark with their head attached, and who is not. The woman who is normal by day but whose neck shows faint marks, who is thin despite eating, who wakes always before dawn, is watched carefully. She may know she is watched. Her expression in the morning, when she comes in from outside and reattaches, does not always suggest that she regrets what she does in the dark.
