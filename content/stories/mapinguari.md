The indigenous peoples of the Amazon basin — the Kayapó, the Kanamari, the communities of the Mato Grosso and the Bolivian lowlands — have described it consistently for centuries: a creature the height of a man but much wider, covered in thick reddish fur, walking upright on feet that point backward, with claws that can tear through the bark of large trees and a mouth located in its abdomen rather than its face. The smell precedes it. Hunters who have encountered it say the odor is so powerful it functions as a weapon — a cloud of rot and musk that causes nausea and disorientation and has been reported to immobilize prey. In some accounts the smell alone can kill a small animal.

What cryptozoologists and some paleontologists have found interesting about the Mapinguari accounts is their potential correspondence with *Megatherium* and related species of giant ground sloth, which disappeared from South America at the end of the Pleistocene — roughly ten thousand years ago, give or take. A surviving ground sloth would be approximately the right size, would have approximately the right body plan, and the backward-pointing feet are consistent with what fossil evidence suggests about ground sloth locomotion. The possibility that the Mapinguari is a relict population of a species presumed extinct, preserved in the deep Amazon where ecological conditions have remained largely unchanged since the Pleistocene, is discussed seriously by a small number of researchers.

The tradition adds elements that go beyond any known biology. The Mapinguari's skin is sometimes described as impenetrable — bullets and arrows deflect from it. The single eye that appears in some accounts places it in a different category from any sloth. And its behavior, as indigenous accounts describe it, is not that of an animal. It is deliberate. It tracks specific people. It returns to the same locations over years. It seems to understand, in some accounts, that it is being hunted.

In the interior communities where the accounts are most detailed and most consistent, the Mapinguari is treated with the seriousness given to genuine danger. Hunters encountering its signs — the torn trees, the enormous footprints, the smell that lingers in the undergrowth — do not pursue. They note the location and leave it well alone. The Amazon is large enough and deep enough that whatever lives in its center does so without needing to be classified.
