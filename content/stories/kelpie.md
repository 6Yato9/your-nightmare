Every loch in Scotland has one. This is understood. The kelpie is the horse-shaped creature that lives in the deep water of freshwater lochs, and it has been drowning people for as long as people have lived near the water, which is to say since the beginning. It appears on the banks of a loch in the form of a beautiful horse — large, well-fed, its coat gleaming, its manner calm and gentle enough to suggest it has been tamed. Sometimes it is saddled, which is part of the trap. A lost traveler finding a saddled horse grazing unattended by a loch at dusk would be forgiven for seeing good fortune.

The trap is in the skin. Once you have touched the kelpie's back, once you have swung a leg over and settled into the saddle, the adhesion begins. Your hands stick to its mane. Your legs stick to its sides. There is no dismounting from this point. The kelpie moves toward the water and the water opens for it, and it goes down, and you go with it, and the loch closes above you both. The following morning, if the water is shallow enough, parts of the victim wash up. Specifically, the organs, sometimes the liver. The rest stays below.

In some accounts, particularly from the Highlands, the kelpie can take human form: a beautiful man or woman who appears wet and will not explain why, who makes advances that move too quickly, who smells faintly of pondweed. In the human form, a clue is available to those who know to look — there are always traces of water plants in the hair, always the slightly wrong quality of cold that the skin gives off, always the way the eyes hold still when the expression changes, the animation not quite continuous. Some stories describe women recognizing the kelpie in its human form and escaping. Some describe women who did not recognize it and did not escape.

The traditional countermeasure is a bridle with an iron bit — if you can get an iron bridle onto a kelpie, it becomes docile and can be worked like an ordinary horse. The supernatural strength of the kelpie, harnessed this way, was sometimes used in legend to pull heavy loads that ordinary horses couldn't manage. But the bridle must be on before you touch the skin. After that, the only thing between you and the loch floor is how long you can hold your breath, and the kelpie is patient, and the water is very deep.
