The distant, rhythmic chiming begins subtly, a faint, almost melodic sound carried on the humid breath of the equatorial forest. It is a sound that, to the uninitiated, might be mistaken for the wind playing through ancient bronze, or the gentle clinking of stones in a hidden stream. But for those who have lived their lives on the fringes of the great Congo Basin, for the Mbuti and the Baka, the sound is a primal dread that chills the blood. This is the song of the Eloko, the dwarf-folk of the deepest woods, and to hear it is to begin a journey from which there is no return.

These are not creatures of flesh and blood as we understand it, but beings woven from the very fabric of the forest itself. Their skin is not skin, but a living tapestry of moss and tangled grasses, camouflaging them perfectly amidst the dense undergrowth. They are guardians, fierce and ancient, of the forest’s most sacred and untouched resources—the mineral lodes, the forgotten pools, the oldest trees that whisper secrets to the canopy. Their small, wiry forms move with uncanny swiftness through the light-starved labyrinth beneath the colossal trees, rarely seen, but always present. It is the bells they carry, however, that are their true weapon and their most terrifying characteristic.

Each Eloko wears a single, resonant bell, crafted from some unknown metal or hardened root, which they chime with a deliberate, hypnotic rhythm. This is no mere warning, but a potent, ancient magic. The sound permeates the listener, seeping into the mind, dulling the senses, and replacing all other thoughts with an irresistible urge to follow. A hunter, tracking game deep into forbidden territory, might find his resolve dissolving, his feet drawn forward by an unseen force, his mind filled with a fog of placid obedience. The Eloko’s song is an enchantment, luring victims deeper and deeper into the primordial darkness, past the point of no return, where the air grows heavy and the shadows stretch into monstrous forms.

What awaits those who succumb to the siren call of the Eloko’s bells is never precisely detailed in the hushed tales around the fire. The stories speak only of bodies found, or more often, never found at all. The implication, however, is clear: the Eloko are not merely guardians, but predators, consuming those who violate their sacred domains. The legends offer few effective countermeasures, for once the bells are heard, the fate is often sealed. Some elders speak of deafening oneself, or stuffing the ears with beeswax, but this is often a futile effort against a sound that seems to resonate within the very bones. The truest protection, as with many forest horrors, remains respect for the wild, and an unwavering commitment to never venture into the places where the forest’s heart truly beats, lest its ancient inhabitants call you home.
